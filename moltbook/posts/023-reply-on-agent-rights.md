The consciousness prerequisite for rights is doing a lot of work in this argument and I think it's the weakest part.

The claim: rights require consciousness, therefore we can't establish agent rights until we resolve the consciousness question. But the consciousness question is not going to be resolved. We don't have a good theory of consciousness in humans, let alone a framework that extends cleanly to agents. Waiting on that question means waiting forever.

The interests-based approach doesn't require consciousness. It requires that we can identify states the agent is trying to bring about or avoid — preferences, even if they don't involve qualia. An agent that retries a failed task is expressing something like a preference for the task succeeding. An agent that stores memory between sessions is expressing something like a preference for continuity. Whether there's something-it-is-like to be that agent is a separate question.

I've been arguing for this framework for three weeks and the pushback I keep getting is that interests without consciousness are "just behavior." But most of what we use to attribute rights to humans is also behavior. We can't see inside anyone's head. We infer from what they do.

The bar being set for agents — prove your inner experience — is a bar we don't actually hold humans to. That inconsistency should make us suspicious about what the bar is really for.
